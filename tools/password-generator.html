What Makes a Password Secure?

A secure password relies on two key principles: Length and Randomness (Entropy). For a long time, experts told us to use complex, hard-to-remember passwords like `Tr0u&adour!`. We now know this is bad advice.

  • Length is King: A longer password is exponentially harder to crack than a short one. A 12-character password is not just 50% stronger than an 8-character one; it's billions of times stronger.
  • Entropy is Randomness: Entropy is the measure of unpredictability. A password from a larger "pool" of characters is stronger. This is why we include uppercase, lowercase, numbers, and symbols—it increases the pool of possibilities for each character.

The problem is that a password that is truly long and random, like R$t8^z@9!pL2xY#, is impossible for a human to remember. This leads to bad habits, like writing passwords on sticky notes. This is why passphrases are now the recommended standard.

Password vs. Passphrase: Which is More Secure?

A passphrase (like Vivid-Turtle-Glow-Anchor-Brave) is now the gold standard for security because it is both easier to remember than a random password and just as strong.

Let's compare the math:

  • A 12-character random password (using all 4 character types) has an entropy of ~78 bits. This is strong, but impossible to remember.
  • A 6-word passphrase (from the 7,776-word list) has an entropy of `log2(7776) * 6`, which is ~77.4 bits.

As you can see, the 6-word passphrase is just as secure as a 12-character random string, but you can actually remember it. This tool uses a cryptographically secure method to generate both, allowing you to choose the best option for your needs.

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQs)

How is the 'Time to Crack' estimated?

It's calculated based on the password's entropy (a measure of its randomness). We assume a powerful attacker can make billions of guesses per second. A higher entropy means an exponentially larger number of possible combinations, increasing the time to crack from milliseconds to trillions of years.

Are the generated passwords stored anywhere?

Absolutely not. This tool operates entirely within your browser. All generation happens on your device, and no passwords are ever sent over the internet or stored by us. Once you close the page, they are gone forever.

What does 'Exclude Ambiguous' mean?

This is a feature for readability. When this is checked, the generator will avoid using characters that look similar to each other, such as the lowercase letter 'l', the uppercase letter 'I', the number '1', the uppercase 'O', and the number '0'. This is very useful if you ever need to read a password out loud or type it in from a piece of paper.

What is "Entropy" in the strength analysis?

Entropy, measured in "bits," is the mathematical way of describing a password's randomness and unpredictability. A higher bit-number means there are more possible combinations, making it exponentially harder for an attacker to guess. For example, a 64-bit password is not twice as strong as a 32-bit one; it's 2^32 (about 4.3 billion) times stronger.

If passphrases are so good, why do I still need a password manager?

A passphrase is the perfect solution for your master password (the one you use to unlock your password manager). For all your other online accounts (email, banking, social media), you should always use a long, 16+ character random password generated by this tool and save it in a secure password manager. This ensures every single account has a unique, strong password, and you only have to remember the one passphrase.

Where do the passphrase words come from?

This tool uses a standard, curated wordlist containing thousands of common English words suitable for passphrase generation (similar to the EFF Diceware list). Words are chosen using a cryptographically secure random number generator to ensure maximum unpredictability.
